What do we find?

Theres a lot of fascinating data in here!

  • X,Y co-ordinates, street names, and other geographic info
  • Both calendar and financial year, and holidays if applicable
  • Vehicle counts involved in crash
  • Objects crashed into, where applicable
  • Weather and light conditions
  • Outcomes on individuals: injuries and severity

An initial visualisation

Observations

There are some limitations with this data source.

  • No info on traffic volumes to provide context to crash numbers. Should one avoid Auckland because its where the majority of crashes happen?
  • No date-time information more precise than year
  • Which parties were responsible for the crash, and which parties received the injuries

We also seem to have incomplete data for 2023, perhaps we should drop this as it skews our analysis.

Updated visualisation

This better contrasts the severity of crashes by region. Here we can see crashes are less likely to be severe in Auckland and Wellington. However, it doesnt resolve the crash likelihood issue. Let us say, in a region, 5% of crashes are fatal. How do we reduce that figure? We can decrease the number of fatal crashes, or we can increase the number of non-fatal crashes. Regions with a low fatality rate could actually just have a high rate of non-fatal crash obscuring the number of fatal crashes.

On another note, we notice a dip in crashes between 2010 and 2015 in all regions, what could be driving that?

Crashes involving motorbikes

A report from NZTA noted the increase in fatalities could be driven by an increase in motorcycle registrations.

However, this doesnt seem to be a major contributor to overall traffic collisions. We see dips from 2010-2015 for both crashes involving and not involving motorcycles, and those not involving motorcycles are clearly a larger contributor to overall crashes.

It appears as though people not inside a vehicle correlate most strongly with high severity crashes. This doesnt confirm they are the recipients of the severe injuries, but it stands to reason. In contrast, crashes involving a train are probably more severe for the person on the other end.

We also see bridges, rivers, and bodies of water as high correlates with severity, which makes a lot of sense. Trees, posts, and poles could be because theyre quite sturdy objects, so crashing into them means you come to a more sudden halt than if you crash into a fence, house, or animal.

Ideas for further exploration

  • Link in traffic volumes - NZTA measure this for major roads, Auckland Transport measure for Auckland roads. This can help us identify which roads are disproportionately dangerous
  • Could look at census usual mode of travel to work to estimate cyclist volumes against bicycle related crashes
  • If we find a way to get time-of-day data or estimate it, Id be interested to explore crash rates for people driving into the sun (East in the morning, West in the evening)
  • Also if time of day has an impact more generally on crash likelihood and severity (after accounting for light conditions)
  • Who is responsible in these crashes? Do cars need to be more mindful of pedestrians and (motor)cyclists, or do pedestrians and cyclists need to be more careful?
  • Why did crashes of all severity levels dip from 2010 - 2015?

Actions

  • Consult with subject matter and policy experts
  • Policy, information campaigns to protect pedestrians and cyclists - or to teach pedestrians and cyclists how to stay safe on the road
  • Information campaigns for surviving crashing into bodies of water
  • Transform footpaths to make them safer for pedestrians
  • Airbags for trees? - Maybe not!
